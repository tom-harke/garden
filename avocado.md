# Avocado Growing Guide (Zone 8b/9a - PNW)

Avocados are marginal in the Pacific Northwest, but with the right variety and microclimate, they can thrive. Focus on **Mexican** varieties, which are the most cold-hardy.

## Recommended Varieties

| Variety | Hardiness | Best For... | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Fantastic** | **15°F** | **The most hardy?** | Paper-thin green skin; creamy texture. Often cited as the most cold-tolerant of all. |
| **Del Rio (Pryor)** | **15°F** | **Flavor/Oil** | Small fruit (3-4 oz) but high oil content and excellent taste. |
| **Brazos Belle** | **15°F** | **Purple Fruit** | Medium-large, purple-black long fruit. Ripe Oct-Nov. |
| **Poncho** (Pancho?) | **15°F** | **Hardiness** | Medium-large green fruit. Very cold tolerant. |
| **Lila** | **15°F** | **Hardiness** | Medium-size green fruit. **Note:** Do not confuse with "Lula" (hardy only to 25°F). |
| **Brogdon** | **~20°F** | **PNW Rainy Climate** | Hybrid Mexican variety that **tolerates rain better** than others. |
| **Mexicola Grande** | **20°F** | **Classic Flavor** | Larger fruit than standard Mexicola; dark, glossy skin. |
| **Joey** | **20°F** | **Self-Fertility** | Selected in Uvalde, TX. Heavy producer; egg-shaped purple fruit. |
| **Stewart** | **20°F** | **Small Yards** | Resembles Mexicola but is more **compact**. |

## Essential Growing Conditions

*   **Humidity & Rain Warning:** Humidity plays a massive role in cold tolerance. A tree that handles 15°F in dry Texas may only handle **15-20°F in a wet winter** (like the PNW).
*   **Sunlight:** Full sun (6–8+ hours). Protect young trunks from sunburn.
*   **Drainage (The Most Important Factor):** Avocados are extremely sensitive to over-watering during cold spells. There is no faster way to kill them than wet roots in winter.
    *   **Action:** You **MUST** plant on a **12-18 inch mound** or raised bed to ensure drainage.
*   **Siting:** 
    *   Plant on the **South or Southeast** side of the house.
    *   **Wind Protection:** Cold wind is more dangerous than the cold itself. Use a windbreak or sheltered location.
*   **Self-Fertility:** Most cold-hardy Mexican varieties are self-fertile and have an anise-like scent to their leaves.
*   **Watering:** Very little water is needed during cold weather. Allow the soil to dry significantly.
*   **Mulching:** 4–6 inches of coarse mulch. Mound it around the trunk for young plants when a freeze is expected.


## Growth Habit & Seasonal Behavior

*   **Evergreen:** Avocados are evergreen and keep their leaves year-round.
*   **Spring Leaf Drop:** It is normal for trees to shed many old leaves during the spring flowering period. This is usually followed by a flush of new growth.
*   **Winter Desiccation:** Because they are evergreen, cold winter winds can dry out the leaves (desiccation) while the roots are too cold to replace the moisture. 

---

## Propagation
* **Grafting:** The standard for fruit production, grafting onto hardy Mexican rootstock.
* **Seeds:** Possible for ornamental use or rootstock; seeds must be fresh.
* **Cuttings:** Not recommended/Difficult; notoriously hard to root.

---

## Winter Protection Strategy (The "Arctic Blast" Plan)

1.  **Hydrate:** Water deeply before a forecasted freeze. Wet soil holds more heat.
2.  **Cover:** Use frost blankets or burlap when temps drop below 32°F (30°F for established trees).
3.  **Snow Load:** Shake heavy snow off branches promptly. Because they are evergreen, they catch snow easily, and the brittle wood can snap under the weight.
4.  **Heat:** String **C9 (incandescent) Christmas lights** inside the canopy for supplemental warmth.
4.  **No Fertilizer in Fall:** Stop fertilizing after August to prevent tender new growth that will freeze.
5.  **Drip Line:** Keep the area under the tree free of weeds, which can trap cold air near the ground.
