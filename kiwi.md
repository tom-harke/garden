# Kiwis - *Actinidia*

Vigorous perennial vines that are exceptionally well-suited to the Pacific Northwest. There are two primary types grown in Zone 8b: the smooth-skinned **Hardy Kiwi** and the traditional store-bought **Fuzzy Kiwi**.

## 1. Hardy Kiwi (*Actinidia arguta*)
These are massive, vigorous vines that produce small, grape-sized fruits with smooth, edible skin. They are much hardier than fuzzy kiwis and thrive in the PNW.

### **Varieties in the Garden**
*   **Anna (Ananasnaya) [Female]:** The gold standard for flavor. Features a pineapple-like aroma and green fruit with a red blush.
*   **Ken's Red [Female]:** A hybrid variety with solid red skin and flesh. Features a complex, spicy-berry flavor. Slightly less cold-hardy than Anna.
*   **Cordifolia [Female]:** Named for its heart-shaped leaves. Known for being the sweetest of all hardy kiwis with a honey-like flavor. Ripens earlier than Anna.
*   **Meader [Male]:** An essential pollinator for the female vines. One male can pollinate up to 8 females.

### **Other Notable Varieties**
*   **Female:** Fortyniner, MSU, Dumbarton Oaks (early ripening), Geneva, NA-7.
*   **Male:** Clark, Fairchild, 74-32.

## 2. Fuzzy Kiwi (*Actinidia deliciosa*)
The traditional supermarket type with fuzzy skin that requires peeling. These are less cold-hardy than *A. arguta* and require more heat to ripen.

### **Notable Varieties**
*   **Female:** Saanichton (popular in BC/PNW), Elmwood (high productivity), Vincent (low chill requirement).
*   **Male:** Fuzzy Male.

## Care & Growing Conditions (Zone 8b)
*   **Pollination:** **Critical.** Kiwis are dioecious (separate male and female plants). You must have a male of the same species to get fruit. 
    *   *Note:* Hardy and Fuzzy kiwis do **NOT** cross-pollinate each other.
*   **Sun:** Full sun to partial shade. In the PNW, full sun is best for fruit production and sugar development.
*   **Soil:** Requires rich, well-drained soil. They are sensitive to "wet feet" and root rot during rainy winters.
*   **Water:** **High.** These are thirsty vines. Drought stress during fruit set will cause berries to drop.
*   **Support:** Requires a **very heavy-duty trellis** or pergola. Mature vines are extremely heavy and can easily break weak supports.
*   **Pruning:** Mandatory. Vines can grow 15–20 feet in a single season. Prune heavily in winter to establish a permanent structure and "hack back" summer water sprouts to maintain airflow and light.

## Propagation
*   **Softwood Cuttings:** Take 6-inch cuttings of new growth in July. Use rooting hormone and maintain high humidity.
*   **Hardwood Cuttings:** Take dormant cuttings in late winter.
*   **Simple Layering:** Pin a flexible low vine to the ground in spring; it will root at the node by autumn.

## Challenges
*   **Frost:** While the vines are hardy, new spring growth is very tender and can be damaged by late frosts.
*   **Vigor:** They are aggressive growers and can quickly overwhelm nearby plants or structures if not pruned.
