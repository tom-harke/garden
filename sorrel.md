# Sorrel - *Rumex*

A vigorous, early-emerging perennial herb prized for its sharp, bright, lemony flavor.

## Varieties in the Garden
*   **Garden Sorrel (*Rumex acetosa*):** The classic hardy variety with large, arrow-shaped leaves. Excellent for cooking (sorrel soup).
*   **Red-Veined Sorrel (*Rumex sanguineus*):** A striking ornamental with deep burgundy veins. While edible, it is best harvested when very young as mature leaves can be tough and bitter.

## Other Varieties (Not in Garden)
*   **French Sorrel (*Rumex scutatus*):** Smaller leaves with a more refined citrus flavor; slightly more drought-tolerant.

## Care (Zone 8b/9a)
*   **Light:** Full sun to partial shade. Afternoon shade in summer prevents the leaves from becoming tough and bitter.
*   **Soil:** Prefers rich, humusy, and moisture-retentive soil. 
*   **Water:** Needs **consistent moisture**. Mulching helps retain soil dampness during dry PNW summers.
*   **Maintenance:** Cut off flower stalks as soon as they appear in June to encourage continuous leaf production. Divide the clump every 3-4 years.

## Propagation
*   **Seeds:** Very easy to grow from seed; sow directly in the garden in early spring or late summer for quick results.
*   **Division:** Established clumps should be divided every 3-4 years in spring or autumn to rejuvenate the plant and maintain leaf quality.

## Edibility & Harvest
*   **Flavor Profile:** Intensely lemony and tangy.
*   **Usage:** Young leaves are excellent in salads. Mature leaves are traditionally used in sorrel soup or paired with rich fish like salmon.
*   **Safety Note:** High in oxalic acid (similar to spinach and rhubarb); consume in moderation. Avoid cooking in cast iron or aluminum as the acid reacts with the metal.
