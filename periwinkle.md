# Periwinkle - *Vinca minor*

Also known as **Creeping Myrtle**, this is a robust, evergreen groundcover prized for its ability to thrive in shaded "problem areas" and its cheerful blue-violet flowers.

## Growth Habit
*   **Size:** Very low-growing, reaching only **3–6 inches tall**.
*   **Spread:** Spreads vigorously via trailing stems that root at the nodes. Can form a dense, weed-suppressing carpet over time.
*   **Foliage:** Small, glossy, dark green evergreen leaves.

## Care in Zone 8b (Portland)
*   **Hardiness:** Fully hardy. It remains green throughout Portland's winters.
*   **Light:** **Partial to Full Shade.** In the PNW, it can handle morning sun, but intense afternoon summer sun will often yellow or "scorch" the leaves.
*   **Soil:** Highly adaptable. Thrives in rich, well-drained soil but can survive in clay or sandy soil as long as it isn't waterlogged.
*   **Water:** Needs regular water to establish. Once mature, it is quite **drought-tolerant**, though it looks plusher with occasional supplemental water during dry summers.

## Propagation
*   **Division:** The easiest method. Simply dig up a rooted section of the trailing stems and transplant it to a new location.
*   **Cuttings:** Softwood cuttings taken in spring or summer root very easily in water or moist soil.

## Growth Habit: The "Sleep, Creep, Leap" Rule
Periwinkle is famous for being slow to start. If you recently planted it, follow this timeline:
*   **Year 1 (Sleep):** The plant focuses entirely on root establishment. It may look like it’s doing nothing or even slightly declining.
*   **Year 2 (Creep):** You will see a few trailing runners starting to move outward.
*   **Year 3 (Leap):** The plant "explodes" and begins to form the dense carpet it's known for.

## Why Your Periwinkle Might Be Unhappy
If your periwinkle is yellowing, slow-growing, or wilting, it is likely due to:
1.  **Too Much Sun:** If it's in a south-facing or unshaded spot, the PNW summer sun may be too intense.
2.  **Poor Drainage:** While it likes moisture, sitting in waterlogged clay during a wet Portland winter can lead to root rot.
3.  **Vinca Blight (*Phomopsis livia*):** Common in the wet PNW. Stems will turn dark brown or black at the base and wilt. If you see this, prune out the infected stems and improve airflow.
4.  **Establishment Phase:** If it's in its first year, it is likely just in the "Sleep" phase and focusing on roots rather than leaves.

## Ideal Relocation Spots
*   **Understory:** Beneath large trees (like Maples or conifers) where grass won't grow.
*   **Shaded Banks:** Excellent for erosion control on slopes that don't get direct afternoon sun.
*   **North-Facing Walls:** A perfect "filler" for the narrow, shaded strips along the north side of a house or fence.
