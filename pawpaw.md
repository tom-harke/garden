= Pawpaw (Asimina triloba)
== Overview
Also known as the "Indiana Banana" or "American Custard Apple." Native to North America. Features large, drooping leaves that give a tropical appearance.

== Characteristics
 - *Zones:* 5-8 (Perfectly hardy in 8b/9a).
 - *Height:* 15–30 feet.
 - *Fruit:* Soft, creamy texture with a sweet, custard flavor.
 - *Fall Color:* Leaves turn bright yellow.

== Varieties
 - *Native Pawpaw:* Grown from wild-harvested seeds.
 - *Select Pawpaw:* Improved cultivars (often grafted or selected for superior fruit).
 - *PNW Recommended:* In Zone 8b/9a, choose early-ripening varieties to ensure fruit matures before the rainy season.
    - **Pennsylvania Golden:** Very reliable for the Northwest; earliest ripening.
    - **Shenandoah:** Prized for flavor and consistent production.
    - **Sunflower:** Large fruit; partially self-fertile (but still needs a partner).

== Care Notes
*   **Sun:** **Full Sun (Mature) / Shade (Young).** Young trees (years 1–2) will die in direct sun. Provide a shade structure until established. Mature trees prefer full sun.
*   **Water:** **High.** Pawpaws are native to river bottomlands and need consistently moist, well-drained soil. Do not let them dry out during summer.
*   **Pollination:** **Not self-fertile.** You must plant at least two different varieties for fruit set.
*   **Soil:** Prefers rich, well-drained, loamy soil.
*   **Pests:** Naturally disease-resistant and has excellent pest resistance.
*   **Wildlife:** Supports native pollinators; deer will eat fallen fruit but generally leave the trees alone.

== Propagation
*   **Seeds:** The most common method; requires 60–100 days of cold, moist stratification. Never let seeds dry out as it kills the embryo.
*   **Grafting:** Necessary for replicating specific cultivars; chip-budding or whip-and-tongue grafts are most successful in spring.
*   **Cuttings:** Extremely difficult and not recommended for home propagation.
