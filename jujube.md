# Jujube - *Ziziphus jujuba*

A highly pest-resistant, drought-tolerant fruit tree that produces fruit often described as "mini apples" when fresh and "dates" when dried. 

## PNW Suitability (Zone 8b/9a)
The primary challenge in the Pacific Northwest is not the cold, but the **lack of summer heat**. Jujubes require high heat units to set and ripen fruit. To succeed in Zone 8b, you must prioritize **early-ripening varieties** and use **heat-trapping techniques**.

### Recommended Varieties for PNW
These varieties are selected for their ability to ripen with fewer heat units and shorter growing seasons.

| Variety | Fruit Type | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Honey Jar** | Small, round | **Top Recommendation for PNW.** Earliest to ripen; extremely sweet and crunchy. |
| **Sugar Cane** | Medium, round | Very sweet; precocious (fruits early); ripens shortly after Honey Jar. |
| **So (Contorted)** | Round, sweet | Ornamental zig-zag branches; very early ripening. |
| **Black Sea** | Medium, oval | Ukrainian origin; specifically bred for shorter, cooler seasons. |
| **Winter Delight**| Large, egg-shaped | Early ripening despite the name; very cold hardy. |

### Other Common Varieties
*   **Li:** Large fruit, but requires more heat than Honey Jar to reach full sweetness.
*   **Lang:** Large bell-shaped fruit; best when dried. Ripens later than Li.
*   **Shanxi Li:** Largest fruit (up to 2"); requires significant heat.
*   **G-A 866:** Extremely high sugar, but requires **intense desert-like heat**; not recommended for the PNW coast.

## Growing Strategies for Zone 8b
*   **Heat Trapping:** Plant against a **south-facing wall** or fence to maximize reflected heat.
*   **Sun:** Needs the fullest sun available (8–10+ hours). Avoid any afternoon shade.
*   **Drainage:** Must have well-drained soil to survive wet PNW winters. **Raised beds or mounds** are highly recommended.
*   **Pollination:** While "partially self-fertile," planting **two different varieties** (e.g., Honey Jar and Sugar Cane) significantly improves fruit set in marginal climates.
*   **Harvesting:** In cool years, pick when the fruit is **yellowish-green with brown spots**. It will be crisp and sweet at this stage and can finish browning indoors.

## Care Notes
*   **Sun:** **Full Sun (8+ hours).** Absolute priority. Plant in the hottest spot available.
*   **Water:** **Low to Moderate.** Water regularly during the first 2 years to establish roots. Once established, they are exceptionally drought-tolerant.
*   **Bloom:** Flowers bloom in June (escaping late frosts) and have a distinct "grape soda" scent.
*   **Suckers:** Rootstock may produce suckers if soil is disturbed.
*   **Roots:** Check for pot-bound or "J-rooted" trees when receiving from nurseries.

## Propagation
*   **Grafting:** The primary method for named cultivars; use bark or whip-and-tongue grafts on hardy rootstock in early spring.
*   **Suckers:** Dig up and transplant root suckers in late winter, though they will represent the rootstock's characteristics.
*   **Seeds:** Used mainly for growing rootstocks; requires 2–3 months of cold stratification after soaking for 24–72 hours.
