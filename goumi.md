# Goumi - *Elaeagnus multiflora*

A highly resilient, nitrogen-fixing shrub that produces lycopene-rich, sweet-tart berries in early summer. It is a staple of permaculture and food forests in the Pacific Northwest.

## Characteristics
*   **Growth Habit:** Deciduous to semi-evergreen shrub, reaching 6–10 feet in height and width.
*   **Nitrogen Fixation:** Hosts *Frankia* bacteria in root nodules to fix atmospheric nitrogen, improving soil health for surrounding plants.
*   **Flowers:** Small, creamy-white, and intensely fragrant in April–May. Highly attractive to bees.
*   **Fruit:** Scarlet, silver-speckled berries ripening in late June to July. One of the first fruits of the season.
    *   **Flavor:** Very tart and astringent until "dead-ripe" (soft/wrinkled), then sweet-tart like a pie cherry.
    *   **Nutrition:** Exceptionally high in lycopene (up to 17x more than tomatoes) and vitamins A, C, and E.

## Care & Management
*   **Sun:** Full sun for best fruit production; tolerates partial shade but with lower yields.
*   **Soil:** Highly adaptable to sandy, loamy, or clay soils. Prefers well-drained conditions.
*   **Water:** Drought-tolerant once established, though summer watering improves fruit quality.
*   **Pruning:** Minimal. Can be pruned in late winter to maintain shape or as a hedge.
*   **Pests:** Remarkably pest and disease resistant. Netting may be required to protect fruit from birds.

## Recommended Varieties for PNW
*   **'Sweet Scarlet':** A top-tier selection from Ukraine; known for being the sweetest and most productive.
*   **'Red Gem':** Very productive and a great cross-pollinator for Sweet Scarlet.
*   **'Carmine':** Selected for large, high-quality fruit.

## Garden Role
*   **Companion Planting:** Excellent "support species" for heavy-feeding trees (like Figs or Pawpaws) or shrubs (like Blueberries).
*   **Hedge:** Can be grown as a dense, fruiting privacy screen or a windbreak.
*   **Early Harvest:** Bridges the gap between spring greens and mid-summer berries.

## PNW Suitability
*   **Hardiness:** Fully hardy in Zone 8b (rated for Zones 4–9).
*   **Non-Invasive:** Unlike its relative, the Autumn Olive, Goumi is not considered invasive in the Western US due to our dry-summer climate.
